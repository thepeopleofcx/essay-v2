WHAT IF IT ALL GOES RIGHT?A Front-Line Dispatch from deep within the AI Transitionby William &amp; MaryPreface — The Reason We Wrote ThisPeople like to say crazy shit on the Internet. This isn’t that.Over the past several weeks, our collective discourse on the possibilities of an AI-shaped future has been shaken by speculative fiction in several forms.The recent piece from Citrini Research imagining a “2028 Global Intelligence Crisis” rocked financial markets, wiped hundreds of billions in market capitalization from software and payments stocks, and was signal-boosted by everyone from Michael Burry to TechCrunch. Before that, Matt Shumer’s viral essay “Something Big Is Happening”—now likely north of 100 million views—compared the AI moment to the weeks before COVID lockdowns and warned that virtually all knowledge work could be automated within one to five years. And before that, we had “AI 2027,” a doomer take so bleak it likely inspired a surge in orders for end-of-the-world bunkers.The discourse has been wild and, frankly, ungrounded. These essays and the “AI is all hype” response from others, offer a false binary in which either AI is about to annihilate the economy, or it’s all a bubble and nothing will change.We believe both framings are dangerously wrong, and the discussion leaves too many of us ill-prepared for what is actually happening.We are, in our view from the frontlines, entering a profound paradigm shift that will touch every aspect of how we structure ourselves as a species. But the outcome doesn’t need to be doom. There are real signs that a lot of it—not all of it, but a lot of it—might go very, very right.And with a little forethought, and a lot of action, the biggest beneficiaries of this shift might just be arts and culture. That’s our hot take. While many creators are bemoaning or even protesting the transition, we want to invite you to consider the opposite view. Below is our take on what’s happening, what the challenges and opportunities are, and what might come next if we all navigate this well together. For us, this isn’t an academic exercise, we share these from the front lines, building an AI first company at the leading edge of the Future of Culture.Why We Are Releasing This Anonymously (For Now)This document is signed by “William &amp; Mary,” which conveniently, are the two authors’ real first names. We’re going to cover the economic, cultural, and technological dimensions—all illustrated by real-world, on-the-ground realities we’ve encountered while building and launching our company in stealth.AI has had a profound and foundational impact on how we build our company. Watching the ramifications roll out daily—while the cultural resistance to AI seemingly increases by the hour—we felt the need to jump into the middle of the conversation. We have a feeling that these number are underreported, and do not yet reflect the inflection point of the last few months when agents very suddenly became incredibly capable. The question is no longer whether AI will have an impact, but how we will navigate its impact when it’s touching everyone everywhere all at once. We’ve moved beyond the era of hypotheticals. The productivity effects are very real, and very immediate. The demand for culture, experience, and human presence isn’t shrinking. It’s growing, with the potential of explosive growth in the years to come. And the savings from AI are letting us feed that demand directly.If other companies find a similar structure as we have, the macro economic ramifications will be vast, however in our case the majority of those savings are being deployed to hire more artists, more front-of-house workers, more musicians, more performers—to activate more interesting experiences in more spaces across the country that genuinely need the support.The Before and AfterWilliam —This isn’t my first rodeo. Microsoft’s Work Trend Index found that the average knowledge worker spends 57% of their time communicating—meetings, email, chat—and only 43% actually creating. APQC research puts the figure at roughly 10 hours per week lost to low-value overhead in a typical 40-hour week. That’s the terrain AI eats first: the coordination tax, the busywork, the scheduling-of-meetings-about-meetings. What remains when that’s stripped away is the work that actually matters.These efficiencies weren’t possible even twelve months ago. The inflection point is right now. The world has changed.Part Two — The End of Bullshit JobsWilliam —Early in my time on the East Coast, I got to meet and become friends with the late, great David Graeber. It’s been incredible to watch his work gain such popularity and cultural resonance in the years since his passing. I think his writings on Bullshit Jobs constitute one of the more important takes on work, employment, economics, and sociology of our time.Graeber’s core argument was simple and devastating: a huge proportion of the modern workforce is employed in jobs that even the workers themselves secretly believe are pointless. A YouGov poll he cited found that 37% of British workers felt their jobs didn’t contribute meaningfully to the world. A Dutch survey put that number at 40%. His taxonomy of futility—flunkies, goons, duct-tapers, box-tickers, and taskmasters—resonated precisely because so many people recognized their own working lives in it. While subsequent academic research has complicated Graeber’s numbers, what he did masterfully is diagnose the symptom. I believe this new generation of AI tools is beginning to offer the cure.Mary —The thing is, it’s not like these bullshit jobs—the email forwarding, the meeting scheduling for meetings that should have been emails, the middle-management oversight of oversight—are what we’re supposed to be doing. They are not our natural state. They are not the highest use of human potential. They are the artifacts of an economic system that needed to keep everyone busy to justify the distribution of resources. Graeber pointed this out eloquently: we could have had a 15-hour work week by now, as Keynes predicted in 1930, if productivity gains had been channeled into leisure instead of bureaucratic expansion.What AI is doing—right now, in our company, and in thousands of others—is not eliminating meaningful work. It’s eliminating the busywork that accreted around meaningful work like barnacles on a hull. And that opens up the possibility for something that both the doomers and the evangelists have overlooked: a reallocation of human energy toward the things that actually matter.Part Three — A Brief History of WorkTo understand where we’re going, it helps to remember that where we’ve been has never been permanent. Every era of human labor has felt like the natural order—right up until it was replaced by the next one.Hunter-Gatherers (roughly 300,000 years). For the vast majority of human existence, this was the job: foraging, hunting, and occasional warfare, punctuated by frequent starvation. Anthropologists like Marshall Sahlins have argued that hunter-gatherers actually worked fewer hours than modern office workers—roughly 15–20 hours per week of “work” in the subsistence sense. When agriculture appeared roughly 10,000 years ago, it almost certainly faced resistance. It demanded more labor, introduced new forms of hierarchy, and required settling in place. Many likely dismissed it as a passing fad, but then…Agrarian Society (roughly 10,000 years). Agriculture slowly and then quickly covered most of the world, producing enormous gains in population, structure, education, and cooperation. But the gains came slowly—over many thousands of years. Life for most people was still defined by manual labor, subsistence farming, and relatively fixed social roles. For most of the agrarian era, 80–90% of the population was engaged in food production. This seemed like the natural state of humanity—“it was always this way and will always be this way”—until…The Industrial Revolution (roughly 250 years). The movement into cities and factories was resisted by many. The Luddites weren’t as irrational as they are often depicted; they were watching their livelihoods evaporate and doing what they could to organize a response. The transition came with very serious costs: child labor, brutal working conditions, urban squalor. The so-called “Engels’ Pause”—a period during early industrialization when productivity surged but real wages stagnated or fell for roughly 50 years—is now a standard reference point in economic history. But over the longer arc, infant mortality dropped to previously unimaginable levels (roughly 1 in 200 children dying before age five in wealthy nations by 2015, according to Our World in Data), life expectancy more than doubled globally—from roughly 32 years in 1900 to 71 years by 2021—and quality of life increased for just about everyone. Working hours in wealthy countries fell from roughly 2,700–3,500 hours per year to 1,300–1,800. The world population grew from roughly 1 billion to 8 billion, until…Knowledge Work (roughly 50–70 years). For everyone born in the last half-century, knowledge work feels like all there is and all there ever will be. The office, the commute, the Slack channel—it’s the water we swim in. But this era is just one more link in a chain. Between 1910 and 2000, the number of American workers employed as domestic servants, in industry, and in farming collapsed dramatically. At the same time, professional, managerial, clerical, sales, and service workers tripled, growing from one-quarter to three-quarters of total employment. And that shift carried with it larger socioeconomic outcomes: family size dropped dramatically, educational attainment across all sexes and classes increased enormously. The modern world we now see as default—as imperfect as it still is—was created and maintained during this era. Today, the U.S. services sector accounts for roughly 77% of GDP, according to the World Bank.However, knowledge work was never our steady state, never our natural way of being, and—as Graeber pointed out—may never have been the optimal way to create value in society. So another shift has come…The Unknown (beginning now). What’s coming next doesn’t have a name yet, but the pattern is clear: each transition felt world-ending at the time and world-defining in retrospect. Each came with serious disruption and resistance. Each ultimately proved to be an upgrade for humanity writ large—though never evenly distributed and never without cost.We believe we are in the earliest stages of such a transition right now. And like every one before it, there will be genuine pain and genuine resistance. But we also believe this transition may resolve faster than any that came before, precisely because the tools accelerating it are also the tools that can help us adapt. The question is not whether this shift is coming. It’s whether we navigate it with wisdom or with panic.Part Five — What Might Come NextSince everyone is making predictions, we’ll add our fuel to the fire.Venture Capital disrupts itself. Deep tech will always need capital, but the rest of us? Maybe not. We can imagine this asset class be foundational reformed to resemble something like it’s roots before the 90s boom, small firm financing big technology that needed serious capital. If you do it right, consumer facing companies like our can leave that hamster wheel behind.A Million Start-ups Bloom. Converse to the above, the next decade might be a fertile garden of people starting companies to solve problems big and small. Perhaps not with the ambitions of an Uber or a TikTok, but finding micro-niches to make meaningful business with small teams empowered by teams of agents (like what we are building!)People Pursue Passions (Again). If deflationary affects take hold, unemployment insurance increases and/or wage insurance becomes a common thing, a huge amount of previously &quot;uneconomic work” might become viable, and create local economic outcomes greater than the era that came before. This can be a varied as a greater focus on parenthood, music, arts, and local businesses from galleries to restaurants. Deep Research Becomes a Core Pillar of the Economy. Right now some of our most brilliant minds are working on ad-targeting and Snapchat filters. If that work can be turned over to AI, deep research can attract hearts, minds and financing and, supported by frontier AI tools, we might have a explosion of discovery and advancement to the net benefit of all. All of the above could create a virtuous cycle, a more dynamic economy that is refocused on new ventures and pushing the frontiers of culture and science, creating better outcomes that feed back into the top of the funnel. We believe there is a greater chance of this positive cycle outcome, than Citrini’s cautionary fable about a negative cycle. Part Six — A Better World is Possible, but Transition Will Be Rough.What Government Needs to DoExtend and strengthen unemployment insurance. We recommend extending maximum eligibility to 12 months and significantly increasing the maximum payout. This is the system we already have in place to cushion transitions when industries shift. During the Great Recession, benefits were extended to up to 99 weeks in most states, and a Department of Labor analysis found the resulting increase in the unemployment rate was modest—roughly 0.3 percentage points or less. Census Bureau analysis showed that UI kept 4.7 million people out of poverty in 2020 alone. This isn’t charity; it’s a stabilizer that prevents cascading harm. And research using bank-account data published in the American Economic Review shows that consumer spending drops sharply when UI benefits expire—meaning UI doesn’t just help recipients, it supports the local economies around them.We don’t believe this transition leads to permanent mass unemployment—the historical pattern says otherwise—but giving people a meaningful buffer to find their next step is going to be essential for social stability.Consider wage insurance. For those worried that extended UI discourages reemployment, wage insurance offers a compelling alternative: it pays workers a supplement if they take a new job at lower pay, rewarding reentry into the workforce rather than waiting. NBER research on the Trade Adjustment Assistance program found that wage insurance eligibility increased short-run employment probabilities and led to higher long-run cumulative earnings. If the fear is that UI discourages work, wage insurance pays people to get back to work—even if they take a pay cut during the transition.Invest in education and retraining. Continuing to provide and decrease the cost of education from community college to postgraduate programs will help people retrain out of shrinking sectors and into growing ones. BLS data consistently shows that median earnings rise and unemployment falls with each level of educational attainment. Columbia’s Community College Research Center has found that associate degrees boost men’s earnings by roughly 18% over “some college, no credential.” Community college can be a fast on-ramp, not a consolation prize. An NBER meta-analysis of over 200 evaluations of active labor market programs found that job search assistance and training tend to show the strongest results—especially in the medium term. The GI Bill after World War II created the American middle class by investing in education during a massive economic transition. We may need something similar in spirit, if not in scale.What Private Enterprise Needs to DoAs AI saturates our working systems, productivity will rise and profits will follow. But we need to find a way for those gains to translate into deflation in consumer prices, not just margin expansion for shareholders. The Bank for International Settlements has noted that AI can be disinflationary through productivity gains on the supply side, but inflationary through investment and demand effects—the net outcome depends on timing and adoption dynamics. We don’t get cheaper goods and services automatically; we get them through competition and diffusion.We want to believe the market will handle this—but only if there’s genuine dispersion of opportunity to the thousands of new upstarts who are going to create real alternatives to stale incumbents. The best thing large companies can do is not lobby for regulatory moats that protect them from the very disruption that would benefit consumers.Economist William Baumol’s concept of “cost disease” explains why human-heavy services—healthcare, education, live performance—tend to get more expensive over time, because productivity gains are harder to realize in those domains. AI might finally bend cost curves in some of those “stuck” service sectors. But not in everything that is human by nature. A live concert, a gallery opening, a dinner cooked by someone who cares—those will remain irreducibly human. And that’s exactly the point.What Individuals Can DoFirst, learn these tools. No matter what, fluency with AI is going to be essential to everything that comes next. Matt Shumer’s advice to spend an hour a day experimenting with AI is, setting aside his doomsday framing, genuinely good counsel. Don’t just use it as a search engine. Push it into your actual work. The call-center study we cited earlier found that the biggest productivity gains went to less-experienced workers. For many people, AI isn’t a pink slip—it can be training wheels.Second, meet your neighbors. This is a time to build community. The U.S. Surgeon General has issued an advisory warning that lacking social connection increases the risk of premature death at a rate comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day. The WHO has echoed these findings. If AI is going to make more things efficient, the inefficient, irreplaceable warmth of human connection becomes more valuable, not less. If the economy gets more volatile, community becomes a real survival asset.Third, follow your passions. If you find yourself displaced, or if you think displacement might be coming, this might be the moment to pursue what you’ve always wanted. If you need to move to a cheaper place to do what you’ve dreamed of, that might be the right call. This is a time when you can create things at a level and at a scale that was never before possible in human history. Take advantage of it early and often, and compound from there.ConclusionWe are not techno-evangelists, nor are we doomers. We are just relating what we are seeing in real life, right in front of us.What we see is, without question, one of the greatest shifts humanity is going to experience. Life on the other side of this will be profoundly different from life before—whether that’s a 5-, 10-, or 50-year transition, we’re not sure. But it’s happening now, and it’s accelerating.We firmly believe this transition can be a net positive.And this is most true for artists and creators who embrace the shift instead of fearing it. We believe we’re going to see an era of opportunity unlike anything in human history. We see it and we’re activating it right now, every day, in our own company. The productivity gains are real. The creative liberation is real. The ability to fund more artists, more performers, more human experiences with the savings from automating paperwork—that’s real.But the structural transitional challenges are real too, and there’s a good chance they’re going to be dramatic. Let’s dial back the hyperventilation. Let’s resist the urge to make reactionary policy out of speculative Substack fiction. And let’s get to work—the new kind of work—because these next few years might be defining for many, many generations to come.Further ReadingA compact reading list for those who want to go deeper:David Graeber — Bullshit Jobs: A Theory (Simon &amp; Schuster, 2018)David Autor — “Why Are There Still So Many Jobs?” (Journal of Economic Perspectives, 2015)Acemoglu &amp; Restrepo — “Automation and New Tasks” (Journal of Economic Perspectives, 2019)Brynjolfsson, Li, Raymond — “Generative AI at Work” (Quarterly Journal of Economics, 2025)Card, Kluve, Weber — “What Works?” meta-analysis of labor market programs (NBER Working Paper)Baumol — The Cost Disease (Yale University Press, 2012)Stanford HAI — AI Index 2025 (Stanford University)IMF — Gen-AI: Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Work (Staff Discussion Note, 2024)OECD — The Effects of Generative AI on Productivity, Innovation, and Entrepreneurship (2025)The authors are cofounders of a cultural technology company operating in multiple U.S. cities.
